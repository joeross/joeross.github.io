Do We Go to College Too Early?

Jun 8th, 2012 | Comments

Merlin Mann mentioned in passing on episode #69 of his Back to Work podcast with Dan Benjamin, Seven Dwarfs of Bad Emotion, that he thinks we may go to college too early.

I think he may be onto something, although I understand it isn’t realistic for most people to simply defer college until later in their lives.

But it’s an interesting thought experiment: if the me of today (I’m 28, but insert your own age for fun) started college tomorrow, how much better or worse would he do than the me of age ~19?

I don’t know the answer. I’m going to law school at night these days. It’s a very different experience from my undergraduate years. After all, a liberal arts university is full of classes you don’t really care about.

There are still classes I don’t like in law school, but the whole experience is preparing me for a specific career choice. I spent my undergraduate time focused on literary theory and writing. It’s what I love, but nobody told me that it would be so damn difficult getting any kind of job out of it, unless I wanted to become a reporter or work retail or fast food while I worked on my world-changing novel.

I wrote elsewhere on this site about how my first major was computer science, but I moved into the English department when calculus, which I simply wasn’t any good at, and Java class converged. It was soul-crushing at the time, and in retrospect, I probably wouldn’t have had such a hard time finding a job if I came out with a degree and a proficiency in coding. But life is a strange beast.

The nature of job competition probably doesn’t allow for any upward revision of the starting age for college. It’s best to finish college as young as possible and begin to accumulate real-world job experience. But if I began undergraduate work today, I would probably get much more out of the experience. I would certainly try harder, even in classes that didn’t interest me right away.

And my brain feels more attuned to the types of both abstract and practical thinking needed to really learn in a classroom setting. I’m a better learner than I’ve ever been, and it’s kind of a shame that the “me” that went to college didn’t have any of the useful experience accumulated by the “me” of today.

Obviously, time travel is the best solution to the problem of “today me” being more ready to excel in college than “college me.” That solution would, of course, require setting aside the limitations described by quantumn physics and irreversibly altering the timeline.

But who wouldn’t set aside physics and the integrity of time itself to improve a previous iteration of themselves?
