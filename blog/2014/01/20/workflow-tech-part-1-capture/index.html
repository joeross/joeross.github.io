Workflow Tech, Part 1: Capture

Jan 20th, 2014 | Comments

Introduction

I’ve been thinking much about workflow, and trying to piece together my own workflow philosophy. So my first official 500 Words post is going to be one in a series about my workflow methods. Each of three posts will cover capture, cataloging and completion, and how I handle work at each phase.

How I Capture

I capture new information differently depending on what device I’m using. Drafts is indispensable to me on iOS. You open the app and just start typing. Once you’ve written what you want to write, there are myriad ways to use it, by sending to other apps, copying it to the clipboard, or simply closing the app in the knowledge what you typed will still be there when you go back to process it later. It’s a great way to use a lot of apps but, most of the time, only open one. Drafts will need its own article some day.

From Drafts, I send the stuff I want to save to Evernote or Dropbox.

Evernote is a robust note-taking app that has far too many features to list here. My main uses for it are capturing links for use at work, saving music demos and lyrics, storing PDFs, and clipping bits and pieces of the web during research or brainstorming. Features like recognizing text in photos, simultaneously recording audio and typing notes, and total platform ubiquity make Evernote essential to my workflow in so many ways it also deserves its own article.

Dropxbox is similarly ubiquitous, but instead of being a collection of notebooks, its more like your standard files-and-folders system. Its sync is bullet-proof and effortless, which is why I use it on every device I own. Whether I’m writing in Byword or nvALT, editing a quick photo with Pixlr, or sharing files and folds with multiple people across several different operating systems, Dropbox is as essential to my workflow as Evernote.

I used Android for years and I loved every moment of it. I was always trying new custom software versions, or ROMs, with neat unofficial features. And I always took (and, as you can see in the previous paragraph, still do take) pride in using cross-platform solutions whenever possible, so I didn’t have to deal with lock-in later. But since I got an iPhone 5 from my wife, Siri has been really useful in getting stuff captured for immediate or subsequent processing.

In particular, I’m constantly obsessed with task apps, and there are some great cross-platform options I still bounce between from time to time. But at the end of the day, I can hold the home button on my iPhone and tell Siri to remind me to change the laundry in one hour, or to call my wife when I leave work, and she’ll know what I mean and create a reminder. I haven’t typed a single character, and I have either a time- or location-specific reminder set up in less than 30 seconds.

Another app that deserves its own post some day is if this then that, a powerful way to make a slew of unrelated apps and services talk to one another in really useful ways. You make “recipes” in which activity in one app or service triggers an event in another. For example, I use this recipe with the iOS app to log all tasks I mark completed in Reminders.app on my iPhone to a text file in my Dropbox. That way, I always know when I did something if I need to check up on it later. Just like Evernote, ifttt is so integral to my workflow it, like Drafts and Evernote, will need its own article.

Conclusion

There’s nothing cross-platform about Siri, but at the end of the day productivity is about reducing friction and getting things done. And that’s the best lesson I’ve learned when it comes to capture: using the same app on every platform and device is consistent and wonderful, but the most important thing is capturing what you need to capture, as fast as you can, and as reliably as you can. Achieve that in whatever way works best for you, and you’re off to a great start in developing a workflow of your own.

And now I have several additional 500 Words article ideas: how I use Drafts, Evernote and ifttt. But first, stay tuned for the second and third articles in my Workflow Tech series, Catalog and Complete. They should be fun (and they’ll probably spawn even more article ideas).
