The Business Case for Abuse-free Dairy

Dec 12th, 2013 | Comments

This began as a short link post, but I had too much to say, so it turned into an article.

The Asbury Park Press ran an AP story about abuse at a dairy farm prompting Nestle brand DiGiorno pizza and the supplier it bought from to stop getting cheese from that farm.

In a circumstance tragically familiar to anyone who follows news of food industry abuse, undercover video showed “workers beating, kicking, stabbing and whipping sick and injured cows.” The owner essentially pleads incompetence (and I explain why below) as a defense:

Wiese, who owns the 5,000-cow farm in Greenleaf with his brother, said he was not aware of the abuse until late last month.

It’s your goddamn job to make sure you don’t hire abusive scumbags at your facility. I don’t care that you are “shook up” about it.

The good news is DiGiorno and its ownership, Nestle USA, as well as the distributor acting as middle-man between the farm and the food makers, have stopped doing business with the farm.

More on the farm’s policies:

Since 2012, the 5,000-cow farm has required employees who handle animals to review and sign its humane treatment policy as part of the hiring process.

That sounds like advice from an incompetent attorney who thought waving those forms around in a court room, if it ever came to that, would preclude the employer’s liability.

I am obviously morally opposed this, and increasingly uneasy about how little I know about where the dairy I ingest comes from. It’s something I need to take serious account of and decide whether being vegetarian isn’t sufficient anymore for me personally.

But making the moral case to the animal industry has never been very successful, and making the moral case to consumers tends to provoke “I’d rather not know about that stuff” response which is both predictable and understandable, not to mention frustrating.

That’s why I’d like to make the business case for eliminating such abuse:

Actively monitoring animals and employees and spending extra time and money on ensuring an animal facility is free from abuse is the optimal behavior if you’re trying to maximize revenue.

The cows at the farm in this story are dairy cows, and the more healthy cows they have, the more milk they have, the more sales they and the more money they have.

Thus, a passive approach to abuse prevention that amounts to nothing more than a pro forma signature on an “I promise I won’t” document at the time of hiring is the height of managerial and fiduciary incompetence.

If the owner was as “shook up” as he claims to be, he would consider resigning and bringing in someone with the business sense to run an abuse-free farm.
