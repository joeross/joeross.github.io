Nate Kontny’s Draft: Web-based Collaboration, Plaint Text, and Version Control for Writers

Mar 13th, 2013 | Comments

I’ve tried almost every plain text writing environment out there. I use native applications to write long pieces on desktop or laptop computers, but I often long for a great web experience for those times when I’m away from a Mac, or at a friend’s house or something.

Now, I’ve found one. Today, Nate Kontney officially announced Draft. It’s a web-based plaint text writing environment, and anyone who frequently finds themselves writing in a web browser will love it.

I’ve tried a couple of web-based text editors, and even one that can save to Dropbox. I’m not going to link to it here, but it’s easy enough to find. Instead, I’m going to encourage anyone who writes in plain text and wants a clean environment in which to do it on the web to check out Draft.

I’ve been beta testing it for a couple of weeks and Nate has been impressive in his responsiveness to suggestions, especially considering that I’m one of 1,500 people who had been helping him with the beta. Draft supports Markdown, export as HTML, and file management using Dropbox, Google Drive, Evernote, Box, and local storage. Add to those built-in dead-simple version control, collaboration, and the option to drop a few dollars for a professional review, and you’ve got the best web-based writing environment I’ve seen.

Again, you just have to try it. The design here is wonderful, with plenty of white space drawing your eyes and your cursor to the functional bits. Bold colors and crisp typography make it easy and pleasant to use.

I’m not a designer, but I like to write about the topic here, and I think it’s worth saying that my personal position on design is that it should be delightful and useful. One without the other risks either silliness or ugliness. But apps that both delight and prove useful are compelling in the proper sense of that word, in that they compel people to keep using them. Nate nailed that on Draft, and the best part is that he says this is just the beginning.

Draft is an example of my favorite kind of tool, something built:

  1. for one primary purpose, and

  2. because it enables the builder to do something they really want to do

Tools built this way and for this reason tend to remain focused on fine-tuning and usability far longer than average because the builder has some genuine non-economic skin in the game. In other words, people make good tools when they think they’re going to make some money, but people make great tools when they need something they’ll enjoy using every day.

It’s why I love Marco Arment’s Instapaper, far and away my favorite reading app, and it’s why I love Draft, now far and away my favorite writing app.

EFF Will Represent Targets of Copyright Troll Prenda Law

Mar 12th, 2013 | Comments

EFF will represent targets of copyright troll Prenda Law

Copyright trolls sue lots of people to extract settlements from those who can’t afford to litigate in the face of potentially massive statutory damages. Their claims are often facially lacking in merit and instead leverage intimidation and poorly-constructed federal copyright damages provisions to bankrupt people for profit.

I’m impressed by Wordpress’ parent company, Automattic, who refused to respond to the troll’s fishing expedition. And it’s good to see EFF lend a hand here in the form of representation, but eventually Congress needs to step in and fix the statutory damages provisions that incentivize copyright trolls to this vile abuse of our legal system in the first place.

For some great background and reporting on trolls and those who fight them, read this Ars Technica piece by Timothy B. Lee.

How to Export Your Last.fm Listening History on a Mac

Mar 7th, 2013 | Comments

My goal was simple: I wanted to export all of the tracks I’ve listened and stored in my Last.fm account. I don’t have any real experience working with APIs, but thanks to Jeroen Baert’s post, which I found via this StackExchange thread, I found a handy Python script that even a newb can run.

The script was originally written for use in moving your Last.fm data to Libre.fm, but it works just as well as a standalone backup.

I saved lastexport.py to my home folder (the one with your Mac username) and opened up a Terminal window. Then, I just pasted the following command into the Terminal prompt and pressed Enter:

python lastexport.py -u last.fm_user_name

Make sure you replace last.fm_user_name with your own Last.fm user name. The script will store the results in a text file called exported_tracks.txt, located in your Home folder or whatever other folder you saved the script in. The data in the text file is a little messy, but it’s all there.

If you know how to make the data prettier, don’t hesitate to get in touch with me.

ECPA Amendment Would Require Warrant Even for Email Older Than 6 Months

Mar 7th, 2013 | Comments

ECPA amendment would require warrant even for email older than 6 months

Reps. Zoe Lofgren (D-Calif.), Ted Poe (R-Texas), and Suzan DelBene (D-Wash.) are pushing an amendment to the Electronic Communications Privacy Act that would require a warrant for authorities to obtain any email, instead of allowing free access to messages older than six months.

I wrote in January about Google’s decision to require a warrant even where the law does not, so the ECPA’s shortcomings in the digital age (the law is 25 years old) are sometimes mitigated by responsbile corporate policies.

But a legitimate amendment like Lofgren’s would apply Google’s common sense approach to 4th Amendment rights to all such service providers. There’s simply no excuse not to get this done.
