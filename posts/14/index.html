Focus Your Search to Find the Job You Want Fast

Aug 27th, 2013 | Comments

There are thousands of articles online about finding the job you want. I claim to write about productivity here, and sometimes I do. But not often enough, so here’s some advice I wish someone had given me.

Today social media and technology news site Mashable announced their updated job board in a post by Sheffie Elencweig. It reminded of the most important aspect of any job search, a lesson I learned during the job hunt that would land me at business journal NJBIZ as digital content editor.

That lesson is simple: if you know what you want to do, and you are confident that you have the skills, the passion, and “minimum viable experience” to honestly sell an employer on your ability to excel at the job, be specific in your job search methodology.

Decide what industry you want to work in, and target your outreach and resume to that audience of employers.

For example, I poked around on the usual giant job boards when I began my search for New Jersey employment in anticipation of a move. Some jobs were expired, others were out of reach because of experience or qualifications requirements, and a few, as usual, were scams.

There were a few leads, but the going was slow. Then, I decided I wanted to work in one of two industries: legal, using my recent law degree, or digital media, using every other non-law skill I’d developed over the years.

I searched for and began using industry-specific job boards, and first found the listing for the job I have now on Mediabistro, a website full of resources for new and experienced media professionals. Its focus means far fewer jobs to sift through, and far better opportunities.

My targeted resume got straight to the point about what I had to offer a media company looking for a digital editor, just as my legal resume highlighted my strengths in that field.

If you’re struggling to find a job you’re interested in while wading through sites like LinkedIn and CareerBuilder, consider searching Google and asking folks on Twitter what resources are available specifically for that industry.

Focus and specificity in where and how you conduct your job search may get you that interview you’ve been hoping for much sooner than you think.
