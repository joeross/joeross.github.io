HP’s webOS Group Spun Out Into New Company: GRAM

Aug 18th, 2012 | Comments

HP’s webOS group spun out into new company: GRAM

Derek Kessler of webOS Nation wrote this up yesterday. His post included an internal email to employees of the new company, “GRAM.”

It was very evasive, vague, and noncomittal for a internal email, but this part bothered me:

> > Be the culture. Spread our Values: People Matter. Integrity and Trust. Deliberate Innovation. Act small, deliver big. > >

That is a series of bad PR poster captions strung together with no meaningful intent by HP’s Martin Risau. I hope its aimless encouragements aren’t illustrative of the kind of focus, or lack thereof, the new company will have.

webOS has deserved better than what HP has done for it from the moment their acquisition of the unique mobile OS was finalized. Hopefully, GRAM is a new, bolder, and better beginning for webOS.

TrapWire and the Importance of Old-school Journalism in the Age of Leaks

Aug 18th, 2012 | Comments

RT (Russia Today) reports:

> > The employee roster at Arbaxas reads like a who’s who of agents once with the Pentagon, CIA and other government entities according to their public LinkedIn profiles, and the corporation’s ties are assumed to go deeper than even documented. > >

“Abraxas” allegedly maintains and operates a network of encrypted surveillance cameras used for sophisticated facial recognition in antiterrorism efforts.

I don’t know much about RT, but The Economist doesn’t seem too keen on them. The Columbia Journalism Review has a great longform piece on RT that, admittedly, I haven’t read yet.

And while I’m not a journalist, I’m a little concerned that many journalists seem to be running with this story, or at least giving it the credibility of an unqualified mention. Russia Today seems to lack the same credibility we grant to The New York Times and its ilk, but still the TrapWire story ascends. It has hit Techmeme, but maybe it should be on Mediagazer. The alleged surveillance program, called “TrapWire” and evidenced in Stratfor emails released by Wikileaks, might be 100% real. But I have seen many headlines and little if any follow-up reporting or fact-checking of any kind.

For example, no one has mentioned the very public website TrapWire.com, which looks on the surface to be very similar to the “top secret” program described in the Straftor emails. Maybe someone has and I missed it, if so, I’m sorry and please let me know. If it isn’t related to the top secret program, I’m sorry, and let me know that, too. The confirmation on this example is illustrative of the journalism we need in following up on this leak.

I’m worried about the state, and the future, of journalism in the Age of Leaks. Leaks are great as a place to start digging. But they’re not, or shouldn’t be, a place to begin and end an article. I hope a respectable news organization holds off on grabbing for pageviews and publishes an article in a week or two weeks or a month, full of independent investigation and research, that either confirms or debunks TrapWire. That’s the kind of journalism the network world has enabled, and the kind we deserve.
