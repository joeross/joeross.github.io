NJ Superior Court Judge Legalizes Same-sex Marriage in the State

Sep 27th, 2013 | Comments

NJ Superior Court judge legalizes same-sex marriage in the state

> > Superior Court Judge Mary Jacobson granted an emergency request by six gay couples, ordering state officials to begin officiating same-sex marriages on Oct. 21. > >

This is really great news.

But…

As is so often the case in litigation, this is by no means the end of the issue in New Jersey. From the Superior Court, the Christie administration’s lawyers can appeal up to the state Supreme Court if they need to.

One of their arguments, and the one that is sure to be front-and-center in any appellate brief they file, is that the Superior Court lacked subject-matter jurisdiction because the marriage benefits in question were federal, not state, benefits.

It’s worth noting those who get married under today’s decision may eventually find themselves in a legal gray area if a higher court reverses the outcome.

Business Writers Shouldn’t Be Surprised or Upset About Having to Dress Up for Work

Sep 25th, 2013 | Comments

Business writers shouldn’t be surprised or upset about having to dress up for work

Sara Morrison of The Wrap:

> > The [New York branch of the Newspaper G]uild, which represents all Newsweek employees, says IBT is violating federal law by prohibiting employees from discussing their work conditions or criticizing their employer. > >

Okay, that’s a problem if it’s true, since federal law prohibits that behavior by an employer. But the other bit is what prompted me to write a quick post:

> > The report [that jeans and other casual wardrobe choices are prohibited at IBT Media] made waves among journalists, but the outcry might have been premature. Neither outlet that reported on the handbook apparently made the effort to ask IBT employees if it was actually enforced. > >

It’s not enforced.

Even if it were enforced, it’s nothing for serious journalists to complain about. I’m the digital editor at a business journal, NJBIZ, whose parent company requires employees at all of its publications to dress business-appropriate. That means no jeans, real shoes, and yes, even a tie.

Now, I’m new to the journalism world, and I worked in a tie-requiring job for five years before I landed at my current gig, so the professional attire isn’t a new game for me. I am told it’s a little strange to make a reporter wear a tie, but I think there are circumstances in which a journalist just shouldn’t be surprised at the mandate.

For example, working at a business journal, or the International Business Times, one is likely to encounter sources, readers and advertisers any day of the week, whether in the course of one’s own work or as a result of their colleagues’ work.

To be clear, IBT employees weren’t complaining, as they told The Wrap the requirements aren’t even enforced. Apparently, some people were not pleased, though, with the stringent dress code. I think that’s crap.

I can see a music journalist getting annoyed about wearing a tie, but business writers should expect to dress the part.

Silly Emmys Pageview Grab by Forbes

Sep 23rd, 2013 | Comments

Silly Emmys pageview grab by Forbes

I’m sure Ms. McGrath is a decent reporter and human being, as her author page at Forbes.com suggests.

But this article about money lessons to be learned from Emmy-winning television shows doesn’t do her reputation any favors.

It even has a slideshow, which in 2010 Chadwick Matlin, then of the Columbia Journalism Review, now of Reuters Opinion, appropriately called “the scourge, and the savior, of online journalism”.

The Forbes post is plainly an SEO grab meant to capitalize on the wave of Emmys-related searches and entertainment articles that crested yesterday.

I’m not against writing, shall we say, less hard-hitting business posts, but I don’t think referencing the Kobayashi Maru did me many SEO favors. And I’m even okay with maximizing the topics that your analytics suggest play well to your audience, but there needs to be some meat on the story’s bones.

Slideshows are like the zombies of the digital news world: effective at spreading, but lacking in consciousness.

I hope for her sake a shitty editor or a misguided pageviews-at-any-cost editorial policy made Ms. McGrath do it.

And I hope for its own sake Forbes salvages anything left of its own reputation by eliminating such posts from its web strategy.
