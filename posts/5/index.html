An Idea for Circumventing Digital Repression With a Distributed Multi-proxy Network

Mar 12th, 2014 | Comments

From a New York Times opinion piece on the shortcomings of a popular censorship circumvention method:

One popular approach, virtual private networks, allow users in a repressively censored place like Syria to “proxy” the connections through a computer in a more open place like Norway. But when thousands of users connect to a single intermediary, the repressive government notices, and shuts them down.

There must be a way to connect the “thousands of users” mentioned in that quote to thousands of intermediaries, instead of just one.

This is admittedly beyond the limited scope of my technical expertise, but I wonder whether it is possible to build a tool which can turn anyone’s internet connection into a proxy for someone else while maintaining security and privacy for both parties.

This would operate like Tor, except instead of being limited to 5,000 relays, it would be limited only by the number of “host” machines volunteered for use as a proxy.

And what if each packet transaction, or at least each browser session, connected to a different node in this distributed multi-proxy network?

This would allow someone in New Jersey to download software allowing someone in Iran to access the internet via proxy to circumvent the digital repression mechanisms used by the Iranian government.

Tell me I’m brilliant or clueless via Twitter or email.

Kids’ Lack of Bias Makes Them Better Than College Students at Learning New Gizmos. Could It Work in the Law?

Mar 9th, 2014 | Comments

Kids’ lack of bias makes them better than college students at learning new gizmos:

Could it work in the law?

I honestly wonder if this would work for lawmaking as well. I have thought about it before. Can children reach conclusions better than, or at least similar to, those adults reach about which normative values should undergird new law and how those norms should be implemented?

After all, adults bring so much baggage to lawmaking. Personal, political and economic biases undoubtedly inform and even degrade even the most earnest approaches to legislation. Of course, children would likely bring to bear on their legislative efforts some of the biases instilled in them, whether inadvertently or deliberately, by their parents.

But the effect of a child’s lack of personally researched and passionately held practical and philosophical positions on lawmaking is still worth exploring, even if as nothing more than a thought experiment.

If you’re interested in this line of thought, watch this video of kids reacting to gay marriage. The reactions vary, and some clearly stem from parental influence, but it’s extraordinary to watch, if for nothing else than their candor. Congress, ever burdened by political posturing and face-saving, could use an infusion of candor.

What Is Musical Anhedonia?

Mar 9th, 2014 | Comments

What is musical anhedonia?:

The authors of a recently reported study on the enjoyment of music said:

Importantly, what our ﬁndings reveal is not a particular preference for one class of stimuli over another (one person may enjoy opera, while another may ﬁnd it boring), but an inability to derive pleasure from an entire domain, music, which the vast majority of human populations do ﬁnd pleasurable.

I can’t even imagine a life without appreciation for music. Sometimes it’s classical and sometimes it’s dubstep, but I almost always have music going. And while I prefer Rdio over other apps, it usually doesn’t matter how I’m getting the music, as long as I’m getting it.

I’ve met people who may qualify for a musical anhedonia diagnosis though. My father is one of them. Despite having seen bands like Led Zepplin live in his younger days, I don’t think I’ve ever known him to listen to anything other than sports radio. He just isn’t interested.

Music has always fascinated me on two levels. First, I’m easily captivated by the sheer artistry of a moving or motivating song. Second, I like to write music myself sometimes, so there’s a technical aspect to my listening, a sort of criticism of the craft of a song, that’s always playing out when I listen to something.

The coexistence of those two perspectives, as fan and as (wannabe) artist, make music more engaging to me than any other hobby except writing.

Which brings to mind a parallel to musical anhedonia: literary anhedonia. I know people far more intelligent and successful than I who have absolutely no interest whatsoever in literature of any kind. They may read news or blogs, but take no pleasure in a novel or a poem. It’s another state of being to which I simply cannot relate.

Court: Immigrant Brought by Parents Can’t Get Florida Law License

Mar 7th, 2014 | Comments

Court: Immigrant brought by parents can’t get Florida law license:

Until Florida changes its state law on the issue, Jose Godinez-Samperio won’t get the law license he earned.

Unfortunately, the decision appears to follow the law, which is all a judge can (or at least should) do.

But that doesn’t mean I have to like it.

If a nine-year-old’s parents took them along on a drug run and were arrested, would the child be punished for the crime with his parents?

Of course not. So then why can’t an accomplished individual who has fulfilled all the requirements get a license to practice law?

An immigrant brought to the United States by parents who broke the law to do it should not be held liable for his parents’ crime unless we’re prepared to use the same logic to convict the children of drug-runners who were along for the ride.

Are we?

I’m not.

But it’s a state-by-state issue, and the federal government won’t oppose the granting of a law license in to immigrants without citizenship if the state in question has authorized the move. California passed a law in January allowing immigrants who have not yet been granted citizenship to obtain a law license.

The plaintiff in that case, Sergio Garcia, who was brought to the United States while in high school, will therefore be able to practice law.

So, if Mr. Godinez-Samperio is willing to move from Florida to California, and pass a very different bar exam, he will presumably be allowed to obtain a law license and practice the law there.

No, you’re right, it doesn’t make sense. And welcome to the law.

If you’re interested in thinking more on the topic, consider having a listen to a recent interview my former immigration professor Peter Spiro gave recently to WHYY. You can also read Professor Spiro’s blog posts at Opinio Juris.

Why I Moved Back to Wordpress

Mar 6th, 2014 | Comments

I moved my main site at from Tumblr to Wordpress today because I need the latter’s more robust control. I turned to Tumblr for my main site some time ago because it was simpler and more elegant, and those things still hold true.

But much of what I do here is going to be more in-depth and professional in nature, and I just need a better solution for managing that than Tumblr can provide. I’m also considering offering Wordpress site builds and hosting as part of my freelance work, so having my own Wordpress site is a good way to stay up-to-date on the latest developments with the platform.

I still maintain Geekeries at Tumblr for fun images and quotes, and this site’s original Tumblr-built archives are always available at constantandendless.tumblr.com, they just aren’t indexed by search engines and won’t be updated unless I can find a way to mirror this site over there.

If I did everything right, all the old links should point to the proper page over here, leaving Google none the wiser and preserving my SEO rankings.

Enough about me though, (re)commence the writing!
