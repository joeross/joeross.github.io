An Idea for Circumventing Digital Repression With a Distributed Multi-proxy Network

Mar 12th, 2014 | Comments

From a New York Times opinion piece on the shortcomings of a popular censorship circumvention method:

One popular approach, virtual private networks, allow users in a repressively censored place like Syria to “proxy” the connections through a computer in a more open place like Norway. But when thousands of users connect to a single intermediary, the repressive government notices, and shuts them down.

There must be a way to connect the “thousands of users” mentioned in that quote to thousands of intermediaries, instead of just one.

This is admittedly beyond the limited scope of my technical expertise, but I wonder whether it is possible to build a tool which can turn anyone’s internet connection into a proxy for someone else while maintaining security and privacy for both parties.

This would operate like Tor, except instead of being limited to 5,000 relays, it would be limited only by the number of “host” machines volunteered for use as a proxy.

And what if each packet transaction, or at least each browser session, connected to a different node in this distributed multi-proxy network?

This would allow someone in New Jersey to download software allowing someone in Iran to access the internet via proxy to circumvent the digital repression mechanisms used by the Iranian government.

Tell me I’m brilliant or clueless via Twitter or email.
